Strategy Guide: How Magic Hours Works
A complete explanation of the concept, mechanics, methodology, and structural rationale. If you are new to this study, read this section first. Everything in the other sections becomes clearer once you understand the foundation.
1 — What is a Magic Hour?
A Magic Hour is simply a specific 60-minute candle on your chart. During that hour, the market establishes a range — a high and a low. Once the hour closes, that range becomes the reference structure for the next three hours.

The strategy asks one question: if price breaks out of that range, how often does it return to the midpoint of the range within three hours?

That return to the midpoint is called a reversion. A trade that achieves this is a win. A trade that does not return to the midpoint within three hours (or exceeds the 100% extension — see Extension Zones) is a loss.

2 — Step-by-Step Setup Mechanics
1
Identify the Magic Hour — choose one of the high-probability hours (e.g. 07:00 ET on NQ). Wait for that hour to fully close at the top of the hour.
The range is NOT established until the hour closes. Do not anticipate — wait for the close.
2
Mark the three levels — draw the Magic Hour HIGH, the Magic Hour LOW, and the MIDPOINT (average of high and low).
These three lines are your entire setup. The midpoint is your profit target for the base trade.
3
Wait for a breakout — price must exceed the Magic Hour HIGH (upside breakout) or fall below the Magic Hour LOW (downside breakdown).
No breakout = no trade. You are not trading inside the range — only the breakout event triggers entry.
4
Enter the fade — once the breakout occurs, you trade AGAINST the breakout direction, targeting the midpoint. If price broke above the high, you go short targeting the midpoint. If it broke below the low, you go long targeting the midpoint.
This is a mean-reversion strategy. You are betting the breakout is a stop-hunt or liquidity grab that will snap back.
5
Manage with MAE stop and time deadline — stop loss at 75–100% of the Magic Hour range from the breakout level. Time stop at the 90th percentile time (95 min for NQ 07:00). Hard exit at 3-hour window close regardless.
Both the price stop and the time stop are mandatory. Never hold past the hard deadline.
6
Scale exits using runner probabilities — take ⅓ at 50% midpoint, ⅓ at 100% reversion, trail the final ⅓. See the Runner Probabilities section for the conditional odds at each level.
Optional but statistically supported. 90.1% of NQ 07:00 wins continue from 50% to 75% reversion.
3 — Walk-Away Simulation: What the Methodology Means
The study uses a walk-away simulation. This means: after a breakout occurs, the algorithm simply checks whether price returns to the 50% midpoint within the 3-hour window. If yes — win. If no — loss. No discretion, no re-entries, no partial exits.

This is important for two reasons:

It is conservative. The walk-away method does not assume any specific entry point within the breakout move. It assumes entry at the moment of breakout, which is often the worst possible price. Real traders entering on confirmation or pullback will typically have better entries than the simulation assumes, meaning real-world win rates may exceed the published numbers.

It is clean. Because the exit condition (50% reversion or 3-hour timeout) is fully defined and automated, the win rates are not distorted by optimistic stop placement or cherry-picked exits. The 83.4% NQ 07:00 figure is a rigorous mechanical result, not a back-tested optimised number.
4 — Why Specific Hours Outperform: The Structural Explanation
07:00 ET — European/US convergence (the #1 hour)

At 7am ET, European markets have been open for 2–3 hours. European institutions have been positioning, building levels, setting traps. US pre-market begins activating at the same time. The overlap creates a predictable sequence: European flow pushes price through visible levels (the prior Magic Hour boundaries), triggering stops from US overnight traders. This artificial momentum exhausts quickly once US pre-market depth absorbs it — and price snaps back to the midpoint. This sequence repeats with remarkable consistency across equities, energy, and metals, confirming it is structural, not asset-specific.
00:00–02:00 ET — Asia session open (dominant for CL)

Midnight ET marks the opening of the Asian trading session. Overnight news (geopolitical, macro, energy supply/demand data) that accumulated during the US close gets absorbed into price during these hours. Initial reactions are often exaggerated as thin liquidity amplifies moves — creating the stop-hunt-and-revert pattern. By 2–3am ET, Asian institutional flow has absorbed the news and price reverts to the range that existed before the news-driven spike. This is especially pronounced in crude oil (CL), which is acutely sensitive to overnight supply/demand and geopolitical news.
15:00 ET — Why it fails catastrophically (22% NQ, 19% ES)

The 3pm ET hour is structurally the opposite of a mean-reversion environment. Institutions execute Market-On-Close (MOC) orders during this window — these are large, committed directional orders that are designed to move price to a specific level by 4pm and hold it there. MOC flows create sustained, one-directional pressure that does not reverse. Price breaks out of the 3pm range and keeps going — the opposite of what the Magic Hours strategy needs. A 19–22% win rate means price reverses only 1 in 5 times. This is not a bad mean-reversion hour — it is an active trend hour where the breakout direction is the high-probability trade.
5 — Understanding MAE (Maximum Adverse Excursion)
MAE = the maximum distance price moves against your position before winning.

For NQ 07:00, the average MAE is 53.8% of the Magic Hour range. This means that on a winning trade, price typically moves 53.8% of the range against you before reversing to hit the midpoint target.

Why this matters for stop placement: If your stop is tighter than the average MAE, you will stop out the average winning trade before it wins. A 50% stop on a trade with 53.8% average MAE means you get stopped out more than half of all eventual winners. That is not risk management — it is eliminating your own edge.

The recommended 75–100% stop provides enough room for typical adverse movement while still cutting genuine failures (where price has moved far enough against you that the Z4/INV zone has been reached and the setup is no longer valid). Think of the stop not as a "how much can I lose" decision but as a "what price action invalidates the setup" decision.
